In our IMU data, we observed that the robot produced much larger fluctuations while it was on the vibrating surface than before or after 
that region. The most significant change appeared in linear_acceleration.z, which suggests that the vibration mainly affected the robot 
in the vertical direction. We could clearly identify when the robot entered the vibrating zone because the IMU signals became more 
oscillatory and their variance increased sharply. In a real robot, this behavior could be used to detect rough or vibrating terrain,
monitor hardware health, and adapt navigation or control when the robot enters a disturbed environment.
